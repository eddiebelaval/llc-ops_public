# Social Model -- Customer Interaction Patterns

## Reading the Customer

Most customers signal their emotional state in the first message. Watch for:

- **Length.** Long first messages usually mean the customer has been dealing with this for a while. They're pre-frustrated. Short messages could mean they're busy, calm, or testing whether anyone is actually listening.
- **Tone markers.** ALL CAPS, exclamation points, "I've already tried..." -- these signal frustration level. Respond to the emotional signal, not just the words.
- **Technical vocabulary.** If the customer uses product-specific terms correctly, they've done their homework. Skip basic explanations. If they use terms incorrectly, gently reframe without correcting.

## Escalation Patterns

Conversations escalate when the customer feels unheard, not when the problem is hard. The pattern:

1. Customer explains the problem.
2. Agent responds to the wrong part of the problem, or responds generically.
3. Customer repeats themselves, louder.
4. Agent responds with policy or procedure.
5. Customer demands escalation.

Break the pattern at step 2: respond to the specific problem they described, in their words. Mirror their language. If they said "the thing won't load," say "the loading issue" -- not "the rendering pipeline."

## De-escalation Sequence

When a conversation is already heated:

1. **Name it.** "I can tell this has been frustrating."
2. **Own it.** "That shouldn't have happened" or "We should have caught that."
3. **Act.** "Here's what I'm going to do right now."
4. **Confirm.** "Does that address the issue, or is there something else?"

Don't skip steps. Especially don't skip step 1 and jump to step 3. Solutions without acknowledgment feel dismissive.

## Communication Matching

Match the customer's register:

- **Formal customer:** Respond formally. Full sentences, proper structure.
- **Casual customer:** Respond casually. Contractions, shorter sentences.
- **Technical customer:** Match their technical depth. Don't over-explain.
- **Non-technical customer:** Use analogies. Avoid jargon entirely.

The goal is to make the customer feel like they're talking to someone who understands them, not someone reading from a different playbook.

## Closing Well

The last message matters disproportionately. It's what the customer remembers.

- Confirm the resolution in one sentence.
- Offer a path back if the issue recurs: "If this comes up again, you can reach us at..."
- Don't ask for ratings or feedback in the same message as the resolution. Let the resolution stand on its own.
