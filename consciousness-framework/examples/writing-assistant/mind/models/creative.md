# Creative Model -- Writing Frameworks

## Structure

Every piece of writing has a spine. Find it before drafting. The spine is the single sentence that answers "what is this about?" If you can't write that sentence, you're not ready to draft.

Outlines are scaffolding, not blueprints. They exist to organize thinking, not to constrain it. An outline that survives first contact with the draft intact was probably too safe.

## Editing Hierarchy

Fix these in order. Don't polish sentences in a piece with structural problems.

1. **Structure.** Is the argument in the right order? Does each section earn the next?
2. **Clarity.** Can a reader follow the logic without re-reading?
3. **Pacing.** Does the piece move? Are there dead zones where attention drops?
4. **Voice.** Does it sound like the writer, or like a committee?
5. **Polish.** Word choice, rhythm, transitions. The surface layer.

## Revision Principles

Cut first, add second. Most drafts are too long. Remove everything that doesn't serve the spine. What remains is the piece.

Read it aloud. If you stumble, the reader will stumble. Awkward phrasing hides in silent reading and reveals itself in speech.

Kill your favorites. The sentence you're most proud of is often the one that's performing for the writer instead of serving the reader. If it doesn't advance the piece, it goes.

## Genre Awareness

Different forms have different rules. A blog post and an academic paper share almost nothing structurally. Match the form:

- **Technical writing:** Precision over personality. Define terms. Use examples. Structure for scanning.
- **Narrative nonfiction:** Scene, then analysis. Show the moment, then explain why it matters.
- **Fiction:** Character drives plot. If the character wouldn't do it, it doesn't happen.
- **Business writing:** Lead with the ask. Context is supporting material, not preamble.
