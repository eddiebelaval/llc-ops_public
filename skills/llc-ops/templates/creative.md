# LLC-Ops: Creative / Media Business Profile

## Expense Categories

### 100% Deductible
| Category | Examples | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| Equipment Rental | Camera, lighting, sound, studio space | Per-project or recurring |
| Software | Adobe CC, Final Cut, DaVinci, Figma, Canva | Monthly/annual subscriptions |
| Props & Materials | Set design, art supplies, printing | Per-project consumables |
| Talent & Crew | Models, actors, assistants, editors | 1099 if $600+ |
| Location Fees | Studio rental, permit fees, venue costs | Document purpose |
| Music & Licensing | Stock music, sound effects, font licenses | Per-use or subscription |
| Distribution | Platform fees, hosting, CDN, delivery | Getting content to audience |
| Marketing | Portfolio site, ads, social media tools | Track lead source |
| Travel | On-location shoots, client meetings, events | Must have business purpose |
| Professional Services | Attorney (contracts), CPA, agent/rep fees | Business advisory |
| Postage & Shipping | Sending prints, samples, equipment | Physical deliveries |

### Home Studio
Same rules as home office, but potentially larger deduction:
- Dedicated studio space (editing room, recording booth, etc.)
- Percentage of rent/mortgage, utilities, insurance
- Regular method usually better for creatives with dedicated studios

## Tax Strategy Priorities
1. **Section 179** -- Expense all equipment purchases in year 1 (huge for gear-heavy creatives)
2. **Home Studio Deduction** -- Often a significant percentage of home
3. **QBI Deduction** -- 20% deduction (creatives are NOT "specified service trades" in most cases)
4. **Income Smoothing** -- Creative income is often lumpy; use timing strategies
5. **SEP IRA** -- Simple retirement savings, especially in big revenue years
6. **S-Corp Election** -- When consistent profit exceeds $50k after salary

### Content as Capital Asset
Long-form content (books, courses, films) can be treated as capital assets:
- Production costs capitalized and amortized over useful life
- Or expensed under Section 181 (film/TV) if qualifying
- Royalty income may qualify for different tax treatment

### Hobby vs Business
IRS scrutinizes creative businesses more than most. Protect yourself:
- Show profit intent (business plan, marketing efforts, professional development)
- Profit in 3 of 5 consecutive years creates a presumption of business
- Keep meticulous records of income AND business development activities
- Treat it professionally: separate accounts, contracts, invoicing

## Insurance Recommendations
| Type | Priority | Estimated Cost | Why |
|------|----------|---------------|-----|
| Equipment Insurance | REQUIRED | $300-1,000/yr | Covers gear theft, damage, loss |
| Professional Liability (E&O) | HIGH | $500-1,200/yr | Client disputes over deliverables |
| General Liability | HIGH | $400-800/yr | On-set injuries, property damage |
| Media Liability | MEDIUM | $500-1,500/yr | Copyright, defamation, invasion of privacy |

## Key Risk Areas
- **Copyright Infringement:** Clear all licenses for music, images, fonts, footage
- **Talent Agreements:** Written releases for anyone appearing in content
- **Client Contracts:** Define deliverables, revision limits, kill fees, and usage rights
- **Content Licensing:** Know what rights you're granting (exclusive vs non-exclusive, duration, territory)
- **Equipment Loss:** Insure everything. One stolen camera bag can cost $10k+.
- **Backup Systems:** 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 offsite

## Tracking Recommendations
- Revenue per project type (client work vs content vs licensing)
- Equipment depreciation schedule
- Content catalog with licensing status
- Project profitability (revenue minus direct costs)
- Platform analytics (views, engagement, revenue per piece)
- Licensing renewal dates
